T4 Hotspot Analysis — Methodology Summary

How locations are scored, ranked, and selected for directed patrol | Command staff reference
Analysis Date: 2026-04-17 Horizon: 2024-01-01 – 2026-03-31 Analyst: R. A. Carucci #261, SSOCC LAW ENFORCEMENT SENSITIVE

1. How Data Is Gathered

Two independent data streams feed every T4 analysis run. Neither is used alone — they are cross-referenced and scored separately before being combined into a single location score.

CAD — Computer-Aided Dispatch

  • Citizen-generated calls only (911, phone, walk-in)
  • 28-day active cycle window + 90-day and YTD lookback
  • Filtered to disorder-relevant call types: shots fired, assaults, fights, disorderly groups, suspicious persons/vehicles, ordinance violations
  • Self-initiated officer activity separated and reported independently — it does not inflate the demand score
  • Unfounded, Canceled, and Checked-OK dispositions excluded

RMS — Records Management System

  • UCR Part 1 violent and property crime reports tied to the same locations and cycle window
  • Two-layer domestic violence filter applied before any scoring:
    (1) case-number blocklist (1,536 confirmed DV cases removed)
    (2) incident-type fallback to catch unlisted cases
  • Only RMS records that geographically match a scored CAD location within the same 28-day cycle receive a bonus — no standalone RMS hotspots

2. How Locations Are Weighted and Scored

Every scored location receives a weighted score built from three layers applied in sequence. Scores are never raw call counts — they reflect severity, recency, and persistence.

Repeat-Location Boost

If a location has 3 or more citizen-generated scoring incidents in the current 28-day cycle, its cycle score is multiplied by 1.25×. This reflects the operational reality that a location demanding repeated response within a single cycle warrants elevated attention regardless of its historical average.

Final Score Formula:

location_score = [ Σ(Tier 1 × recency) + Σ(Tier 2 × recency) ] × location_boost

Locations are then ranked citywide by weighted score — never by raw call count alone.

3. How a Location Is Classified as a Hotspot

Once all locations are scored and ranked, they are classified into one of five categories. Classification is applied in priority order — the first matching rule wins.

ClassificationCriteriaOperational Meaning
Chronic Top 10% of citywide weighted scores for 3 or more consecutive 28-day cycles Sustained, entrenched problem. Warrants CPTED review, landlord/business engagement, or multi-agency coordination.
Persistent Appears on current cycle and prior cycle scoring lists; below Chronic threshold Recurring but not yet entrenched. Primary directed-patrol target.
Emerging Current-cycle score ≥ 2× prior-cycle score, OR more than 2 standard deviations above the location's own 90-day rolling mean Rapid increase in activity. Requires early intervention before it becomes Persistent.
Diminishing Present in prior cycle; current-cycle score dropped more than 50% Activity declining — but triggers a displacement check before concluding it has resolved.
One-off Appears in current cycle only with no prior-cycle pattern Isolated incident(s). Not briefed as a hotspot; listed for analyst awareness only.

Displacement Check: When a location is classified Diminishing, the system automatically examines a 1–3 block buffer around the prior hotspot centroid. If current-cycle activity inside that buffer equals or exceeds 50% of the prior cycle's volume at the original location, a "Possible Displacement" flag is raised. Displacement is a hypothesis requiring supervisor field validation — it is not a confirmed finding.

4. Criteria for Adding or Removing a Hotspot

A location is ADDED when:

  • Weighted score places it in the top 10% citywide in the current cycle AND
  • It has a minimum of 3 citizen-generated scoring incidents in the current 28-day window (below 3 = Low confidence, appendix only — not briefed as a hotspot) AND
  • Confidence band is Medium or High (≥ 3 incidents = Medium; ≥ 5 incidents = High)

A location is REMOVED when:

  • Score drops more than 50% from prior cycle (classified Diminishing) AND a displacement check finds no evidence of nearby relocation OR
  • Score falls below the top 10% threshold for two consecutive cycles with supervisor field validation confirming no active problem OR
  • Confidence band drops to Low (fewer than 3 scoring incidents) and remains there the following cycle

Downtrend Policy (Approved Command Decision): A single cycle showing a score decline does not trigger removal. A downtrend flag requires two consecutive down cycles plus supervisor field validation before a location is removed from the active hotspot list. One cycle of decline — especially in winter months — is treated as a seasonal artifact, not a confirmed resolution.

7. Important Constraints and Ethical Guardrails

This is historical risk patterning — not predictive policing. T4 analysis describes where and when disorder has concentrated historically. It does not predict future crimes or individuals. All outputs are advisory. Field supervisors validate ground truth before deployment decisions are finalized.

  • Location and condition only. No individual is targeted. No demographic variables are used as inputs. Analysis is defensible under the U.S. Constitution (4th/14th Amendments), NJ AG Directive 2021-6 (Bias-Free Policing), and NJ AG Directive 2023-1 (Use of Technology in Policing).
  • Domestic violence excluded. DV incidents are removed before scoring via a two-layer filter (case-number blocklist + incident-type fallback). Household-intimate incidents skew street-disorder hotspot scoring toward residential addresses and are operationally irrelevant to directed patrol for public disorder.
  • Self-initiated patrol separated. Officer self-initiated activity is tracked separately and never counted in the citizen-demand score. A location where officers frequently self-initiate without corresponding citizen calls is flagged for review — not rewarded with a higher score.
  • High-false-alarm call types excluded. Burglar alarms, medical calls, parking complaints, and motor vehicle crashes are excluded from scoring. They do not reflect public disorder demand.
  • Address normalization caveat. CAD and RMS addresses are normalized to 100-block segments. Minor data-entry differences across incidents can occasionally split a real hotspot across two adjacent rows. Use the ArcGIS map for spatial ground truth.
